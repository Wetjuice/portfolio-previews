On Design

The best game
design is invisible.

It doesn't announce itself. It doesn't explain what you should feel or what you should do next. It earns trust by making the right choice obvious — and then gets out of the way.

These are the convictions I've built a career on.

Players are smarter than we give them credit for. Progression systems shouldn't be treadmills. The graybox phase — when the game is ugly and honest — is where the real design happens. These aren't theories. They're conclusions drawn from 16 years of building games that either proved or disproved them.

I'm Michael Berman. I'm a Game Director at Sony Bend, where I'm currently leading an unannounced title. Before that: Deep Silver Volition (Saints Row), Pocket Gems (War Dragons), Tencent, and Petroglyph. The career has spanned mobile, PC, and AAA console — systems design, UX, progression, open world, and creative direction.

What stayed constant across all of it: a bias toward honest exploration before polish, a belief that great systems generate depth without demanding constant content input, and a preference for teams that can hear "this isn't working" without it becoming a political event.

If those values resonate with work you're building — in games or outside them — that's worth a conversation.

Design Thinking

All Eight Principles

01 ⬡
Systems Punch Above Their Weight

"The best cost-to-experience ratio isn't bigger budgets — it's systems that create more than they cost."

When interconnected rules produce emergent behavior, you get hundreds of hours of player engagement from a fraction of the content investment. Build the system once, correctly, and it delivers value indefinitely.

02 ↺
Validate Before You Polish

"Fidelity is a reward for ideas that survive contact with reality. Find the fun first."

The moment you polish before validating, you're spending craft on an unproven idea. Test fast, test ugly, iterate honestly. A graybox prototype that teaches you the core loop isn't broken — it's the most efficient design tool you have.

03 ▶
Ideas Are Abundant. Execution Is Scarce.

"Design isn't about generating good ideas. Everyone has good ideas. Great design is identifying which ones to pursue — then making them real."

The gap between a promising concept and a shipped feature is where real design work lives. The ability to recognise a good idea in the noise, and execute it with craft, is rarer and more valuable than ideation alone.

04 ◎
Leadership Is Selection, Not Invention

"The most valuable thing a director does isn't generate the answer — it's recognise it when the team brings it in, and commit to it."

Leaders who insist on originating every solution bottleneck the team and undervalue the room. Build teams smart enough to generate options you wouldn't have thought of yourself. Then make the call.

05 ✦
Emergence Is the Medium's Superpower

"The most memorable gaming moments were never scripted. They happened because the systems were good enough to generate something nobody planned."

Last of Us is a masterfully crafted story. But it isn't where interactive media is at its strongest. The unplanned ambush, the spontaneous alliance, the emergent story no writer wrote — those happen when systems and social dynamics collide unexpectedly. No other medium produces that. That is the target.

06 ⊕
Sustainable Systems Over Content Treadmills

"A game that needs constant content injection to feel alive has a design problem underneath."

Content-dependent games place all pressure on production and all expectation on the player to consume. Build systems that generate interesting decisions. Let content amplify that depth. Never let it substitute.

07 ◑
A Game for Everyone Is a Game for No One

"Every decision made to broaden appeal dilutes conviction. The games people love most have a clear point of view about who they're for."

The instinct to include one more type of player is the enemy of design conviction. Define your audience specifically. Design for that person with full commitment. Undefined audiences produce undefined value — in games and in every other product.

08 □
Clarity Is Kindness

"Predictable rules. Unpredictable outcomes. If players can forecast what their actions produce, they can make meaningful choices."

Consistent mechanics build mental models. Mental models build player agency. Agency creates ownership of outcomes. Surprise should come from the system's depth, never its inconsistency. When fire always burns and noise always carries, players experiment with confidence.
